What is next for Arch Apps — our expansion research

When we launched Arch Apps last week, we shipped five tools. They are good tools. They do one thing well, collect no data, and cost nothing (or one flat price if you want the premium features). People seem to like that.

But five tools is not a collection. It is a start.

We want Arch Apps to be the place you go when you need a tool that just works — no matter what the job is. That means we need to build more of them. So we did what any sensible team would do: we stopped building and started researching.

The market we are entering

The global utility app market is roughly $6 billion today and growing at nearly 17% annually. People download about 55 billion utility apps every year. It is a massive category — and one that is overwhelmingly served by two business models: ad-supported (your data is the product) and subscription (your wallet is the product).

The privacy-first segment — tools that collect zero data as a design principle — accounts for less than 5% of utility downloads. That is not because people do not want privacy. It is because hardly anyone builds it.

We think that is an opportunity.

Our evaluation criteria

We evaluated every candidate app against five questions:

  • Is there real demand? — Does this solve a frequent, universal problem?
  • Is the category broken by ads or subscriptions? — Can we win by simply not doing what everyone else does?
  • Does it fit our philosophy? — Is it a single-purpose tool? Can it work offline? Does it need an account?
  • Can we build it well? — Is the scope small enough that we can ship something genuinely good?
  • Can we differentiate? — Is there room to be clearly better by being simpler and more private?

The five apps we are building next

After scoring dozens of categories against those criteria, here are the five apps we are adding to the roadmap:

1. QR & Barcode Scanner

QR and barcode scanning is one of the most-used utility functions on mobile. And almost every scannner app on the market sends your scan data to ad networks or analytics providers. A scanner that processes everything on-device with zero data egress should not be a differentiator — but in 2026, it is.

Our version: point, scan, done. Your scan history stays on your phone because there is nowhere for it to go.

2. Calculator (Scientific)

Everybody needs a calculator. Most calculator apps ask for internet permission — not because a calculator needs the internet, but because they serve ads. Ours will not need a single permission beyond what a calculator actually requires.

Does math. That is it. No ads. No data collection. Just numbers.

3. Expense Tracker

Financial data is the most sensitive information most people carry. Every major expense tracker uploads your spending to the cloud. We are building one that keeps everything on your device — CSV export is the only way your data moves.

4. Timer+ (Pomodoro, Stopwatch, Countdown)

Focus and productivity data is deeply personal. Most timer apps sync to the cloud for "analytics" or "backup." Ours will not. Time your work, your cooking, your life — no accounts, no analytics, no distractions.

5. Habit Tracker

Your daily routines and personal goals should stay private. Most habit trackers require accounts and cloud sync. Ours will be zero-account, zero-cloud, zero-data-mining. Just a clean way to build routines that stick.

How we are building them

We are shipping in three waves. The first two — QR Scanner and Calculator — are the simplest and most broadly useful. They will arrive first. The next two — Timer+ and Habit Tracker — follow shortly after. Expense Tracker is the most complex and will come last.

Every app will follow the same rules as the first five: offline-first, zero data collection, no accounts, and a free tier that is genuinely useful. Premium features (where they exist) are one-time purchases, not subscriptions.

The long game

We are not trying to build the biggest app company. We are trying to build the most useful collection of tools on the market — one that earns its place on your home screen by respecting you enough to stay out of your way.

Ten apps by the end of the year. Each one does one thing well. None of them ask for your data.

That is the plan.

— The Arch Apps team

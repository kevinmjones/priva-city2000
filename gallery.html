04 Wave 4 — privacy by replacement

Every app below exists because a popular one abused the same job. We name the category it replaces, what that category takes from you, who gets it, and what it is used for — sourced from FTC actions and investigative reporting. Then we build the honest version: on-device, no account, nothing to leak.

· Flashlight

The most infamous privacy-abuse app in history was a flashlight. We built the honest twin: zero permissions, and literally no networking code to send anything anywhere.

Replaces "Free" over-collecting utilities — Brightest Flashlight (FTC, 2013)
What they take
Precise location and a unique device ID — sent the instant the app opened, before consent, even if you tapped "Refuse."
Who gets it
Advertising networks.
Used for
Ad targeting and resale into the location-data market.
No permissionsNo network codeOffline
· Photo Metadata Scrubber

Every photo you share can carry the exact GPS coordinates of where it was taken. One tap strips that out — entirely on your device — before the image ever leaves your hands.

Replaces The location-leak surface fed by broker SDKs (FTC v. X-Mode, 2024)
What they take
GPS coordinates and EXIF/IPTC metadata baked into your photos — device, timestamp, location.
Who gets it
Whoever receives the image, plus the brokers and SDKs that harvest location signals (X-Mode, Gravy, Kochava handled ~1B devices).
Used for
Re-identification — four location points identify 95% of people — and profiling segments like "visited a pregnancy center."
On-deviceNo uploadNo tracking
· Cycle Tracker

Our flagship privacy app: a 100%-on-device period and fertility tracker. No account, no cloud, no third-party SDK — and nothing on a server to subpoena.

Replaces Period / fertility trackers — Flo & Premom (FTC, 2021 & 2023)
What they take
Cycle dates, symptoms, sexual activity, and the fact of a pregnancy; Premom also exfiltrated precise location and device IDs.
Who gets it
Facebook, Google, AppsFlyer, Flurry — and, for Premom, China-based analytics SDKs.
Used for
Ad targeting — a pregnancy became an ad signal. A 2025 jury found Meta liable under California's wiretap law.
No accountsNo cloudNothing to subpoena
· Local Weather

A forecast shouldn't fund a location-data business. Ours resolves a coarse location on-device, asks a public weather API, and builds no profile of you.

Replaces Weather apps — The Weather Channel / IBM (LA City Attorney, 2019)
What they take
Precise location, collected under a "personalized forecast" prompt.
Who gets it
A dozen-plus ad sites — and hedge funds.
Used for
Ad targeting, resale, and consumer-behavior analysis for investors.
Coarse locationNo resaleNo profile
· Doc & Receipt Scanner

Capture documents and receipts to clean PDFs on-device, with metadata stripped automatically. The only way your paperwork moves is the export you choose.

Replaces Cloud scanner apps — ad-laden, with a malware history
What they take
Images of your documents, receipts, and IDs — plus EXIF/location metadata — uploaded "to process."
Who gets it
The vendor's cloud, then exposed to breach, subpoena, or model training.
Used for
Lock-in and cloud retention of documents you assumed were private.
On-deviceMetadata strippedExport-only egress
· Secure Notes

"Your notes, our servers" is the industry default. Ours is local-only with optional on-device encryption — no account, no sync, no copy you don't control.

Replaces Account/cloud-gated note apps (Mozilla: ~80% of Play "Data Safety" labels misleading)
What they take
Everything you write — notes are an unusually intimate data set.
Who gets it
The vendor's cloud, subject to breach, subpoena, policy change, and increasingly model training.
Used for
Lock-in, and a permanent copy of your private thoughts on infrastructure you don't own.
Local-onlyOptional encryptionNo sync
